AI4RA: The Intersection Between AI and Data

AI-enhanced data observability and analytics for research administration.

Workshop thesis

What is the most important model?

The answer

Wrong!

The most important model is your data model.

AI models come and go, but the way an institution organizes, governs, and shares its data determines whether any of them are useful.

Two themes

Reproducibility and data organization

  • Reproducibility — Can someone else get the same answer?
  • Data organization — Is the underlying data trustworthy enough to act on?

Role 1 | 1:00–1:50 PM

AI and data science: what's what

The landscape

AI vs data science

  • Data science: structured analysis, statistics, reproducible pipelines, dashboards
  • AI / LLMs: language generation, pattern matching, unstructured data, probabilistic output
  • They complement each other — but people conflate them

Demystifying

How LLMs actually work

  • Predict the next token based on patterns in training data
  • No understanding, no memory, no intent of their own
  • Extremely capable at text generation, translation, summarization, extraction
  • Extremely unreliable at arithmetic, factual recall, and anything outside training data

Trust

Accuracy and its limits

  • LLM output is probabilistic, not deterministic
  • The same prompt can produce different answers each time
  • Sounds confident even when wrong — "hallucination"
  • When accuracy matters: use data science. When flexibility matters: consider AI.

Reproducibility

The reproducibility challenge

  • Traditional analytics: same query, same data, same answer. Every time.
  • LLMs: same prompt, different answer. By design.
  • This isn't a bug — but it changes what you can rely on
  • Disciplines that help: structured output, temperature control, version pinning, human review

Human judgment

Where human expertise remains essential

"A computer can never be held accountable; therefore a computer must never make a management decision." — IBM, 1979

AI has no integrity, no professional ethics, no accountability. Someone with professional judgment must always own the final call.
  • Policy interpretation and institutional context
  • Compliance determinations
  • Ethical judgment and risk assessment
  • Decisions that require accountability

Decision framework

Distinguishing AI tasks from data science tasks

  • AI fits when: the task involves language, generation, extraction from unstructured data, or flexible reasoning
  • Data science fits when: the task requires exact numbers, reproducible pipelines, statistical inference, or audit trails — use R, Python, SQL
  • Both together: AI extracts and organizes; data science validates and reports

Three zones of intent

Does it matter how the work got done?

  • Process-critical — Every step must be auditable. AI may assist, but a human owns the reasoning.
  • Human-in-the-loop — AI prepares, a qualified person reviews and decides.
  • Output-driven — Only the result matters. AI can lead; validate with spot-checks.

In practice

RA examples for each zone

  • Process-critical: effort certifications, IRB/IACUC reviews, conflict-of-interest determinations, financial audits, research misconduct investigations
  • Human-in-the-loop: pre-award budget reviews, compliance checks, subaward risk assessments, cost transfer justifications
  • Output-driven: drafting facility descriptions, formatting budgets, generating biosketches, auto-populating sponsor forms

Role 2 | 2:00–2:50 PM

The data lakehouse and data organization

The most important model is the data model.

Infrastructure

What is a data lakehouse?

  • Data warehouse: structured, governed, fast queries — but rigid schemas, expensive to change
  • Data lake: flexible, raw storage — but becomes a "data swamp" without governance
  • Data lakehouse: combines both — flexible storage with structured governance layers on top

Architecture

The medallion layers

  • Each layer adds structure, governance, and trust
  • Raw snapshots are never modified — always reproducible

Shared standards

The Unified Data Model

  • A shared schema for research administration across the GRANTED network
  • Makes institutional data interoperable and AI-ready
  • Silver views map source columns to UDM names and types
  • Gold views provide a single deduplicated answer across all sources
Same question, same schema, same answer — regardless of which institution asks.

Observability

Can you see what your data is doing?

  • Where did this data come from? (provenance)
  • When was it last updated? (freshness)
  • Who is allowed to access it? (permissions)
  • Has it changed since the last time we used it? (versioning)
  • If you can't answer these, your AI can't either.

Governance

Data governance as prerequisite

  • Provenance, permissions, and quality determine whether AI is safe to use
  • Ungoverned data + AI = confident-sounding wrong answers
  • Governance is not bureaucracy — it's the condition that makes automation safe

Contrast

The data lakehouse vs the data swamp

Same query. Same task. Two very different outcomes.

Lakehouse

Well-organized data

  • Answer comes back fast, accurate, reproducible
  • You can trace where every number came from
  • The AI gets trustworthy context — output is grounded
  • Same query tomorrow gives the same answer

Data swamp

Poorly organized data

  • Duplicate records, missing fields, stale sources
  • No way to tell which version is authoritative
  • The AI blends conflicting information into a plausible-sounding answer
  • Different answer every time — and no way to tell which is right

From messy to structured

The work that makes AI possible

  • Cleaning, curating, and organizing institutional data
  • Deciding what's authoritative and who says so
  • Building shared schemas so data can travel between systems
  • This is the work that earns reproducibility

Reproducibility

Reproducibility through organization

  • Shared schemas: everyone speaks the same language
  • Versioned data: you can go back and see what changed
  • Governed pipelines: transforms are documented, not ad-hoc
  • Same query, same data, same answer — across institutions and over time

Prompt engineering

Structuring requests for reproducible output

  • Be specific about the task, role, audience, and output format
  • Provide examples of what good output looks like
  • Use structured output (JSON, tables, templates) to constrain responses
  • Layer context deliberately — system instructions, task prompts, reference material

Evaluation

Evaluating accuracy

  • AI sounds confident even when wrong — your job is to verify
  • Check facts against authoritative sources, not against what sounds right
  • Look for common failure modes: hallucination, outdated info, subtle bias
  • Design verification strategies proportional to the stakes

From data to action

Connecting organized data to analytics

  • Observable, governed data feeds analytics pipelines and reports
  • AI-assisted decision-making works when the data model is trustworthy
  • The lakehouse makes the data AI-ready; the AI makes the data actionable

Decision framework

Adoption and institutional fit

  • Automate: repetitive, low-stakes, output-driven tasks with clear validation
  • Augment: human-in-the-loop tasks where AI prepares and humans decide
  • Leave alone: process-critical work that requires full auditability

Lessons learned

The GRANTED project

  • What the AI4RA NSF project revealed about deploying AI across institutions
  • Shared data infrastructure matters more than model choice
  • Governance work has to precede automation work
  • Cross-institutional interoperability requires shared schemas, not just shared tools

Discussion

What do you need at your institution?

  • Which data sources are reliable enough to become AI context?
  • Which ones are too messy, sensitive, or incomplete to trust yet?
  • What governance work has to happen before expanding AI or automation?
  • Where should a human remain in the loop?
